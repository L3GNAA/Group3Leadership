PMI Project Management Principles

1. Be a diligent, respectful, and caring steward

This principle emphasizes ethical and responsible conduct, treating stakeholders with respect and fostering a positive work environment.

2. Create a collaborative project team environment

Focuses on building strong, cohesive teams where communication and collaboration are prioritized.

3. Effectively engage with stakeholders

This principle highlights the importance of understanding stakeholder needs and expectations, and actively involving them in the project.

4. Focus on value

Prioritizing the delivery of value to stakeholders and aligning project activities with desired outcomes.

5. Recognize, evaluate, and respond to system interactions

This principle emphasizes understanding how different parts of a project interact and how changes in one area can affect others.

6. Demonstrate leadership behaviors

Project managers should exhibit leadership qualities such as decision-making, delegation, and motivation.

7. Tailor the delivery approach

Recognizing that one-size-fits-all approaches don't work, this principle encourages tailoring project management methods to the specific needs of the project.

8. Build quality into processes and deliverables

Ensuring that quality is considered throughout the project lifecycle, from planning to execution.

9. Navigate complexity

Managing the inherent complexities of projects by breaking them down into manageable parts and using appropriate tools and techniques.

10. Optimize risk responses

Identifying, assessing, and responding to potential risks to minimize their impact on the project.

11. Embrace adaptability and resilience

Being prepared to adjust to changing circumstances and bounce back from setbacks.

12. Enable change to achieve the envisioned future

Facilitating necessary changes to ensure the project achieves its intended goals and delivers the desired value.

üí° Key Insight

Successful teams leverage both formal structures and informal dynamics. While formal roles ensure accountability and clear responsibilities, informal roles emerge naturally and are equally important for team effectiveness.

üìà Team Development (Tuckman Model)

üå± Forming

Getting oriented, polite, uncertain. Team members are learning about each other and the project scope.

‚ö° Storming

Conflict, pushback, jockeying for position. Different opinions and working styles create tension.

ü§ù Norming

Agreement on processes, trust grows. Team establishes ground rules and begins working together effectively.

üöÄ Performing

High-functioning, delivering results. Team operates efficiently with minimal supervision and maximum output.

üéâ Adjourning

Wrapping up and celebrating achievements. Project completion and team dissolution with recognition of success.

üìö Understanding the Model

Bruce Tuckman's model describes the natural progression of team development. Understanding these stages helps project managers anticipate challenges and support their teams through each phase. Not all teams progress linearly, and some may revisit earlier stages when new members join or significant changes occur.

üé¨ Example: Mobile App Development Team

Week 1-2: Forming

Five students meet for the first time. Everyone is polite and enthusiastic. "Hi, I'm Sarah, I know React Native!" "I'm John, I can do the backend." They discuss the app idea cautiously, nobody wants to offend anyone. Everyone agrees to everything. Lots of "That sounds great!" but no real decisions are made yet.

Week 3-4: Storming

Tensions emerge. Sarah thinks the UI should be minimalist, but Mike wants flashy animations. John missed a deadline and Amy publicly criticizes him in the team chat. "Why didn't you tell us earlier?" Arguments about using MongoDB vs PostgreSQL get heated. Some team members stop responding to messages promptly.

Week 5-6: Norming

The team has a breakthrough meeting. They establish ground rules: "Updates every Tuesday and Friday," "48-hour response time on Slack," "Vote on major decisions." Sarah and Mike compromise on a "clean with subtle animations" design. Trust builds as everyone starts delivering on commitments. Inside jokes emerge.

Week 7-11: Performing

The team is in the zone! Code reviews happen smoothly, everyone knows their role. When a critical bug appears, they swarm on it without being asked. They're ahead of schedule and adding extra features. Team standup calls are efficient and even fun.

Week 12: Adjourning

Project presentation day! The app demo goes perfectly. High-fives all around. They go out for pizza to celebrate, share LinkedIn profiles, and promise to stay in touch.
